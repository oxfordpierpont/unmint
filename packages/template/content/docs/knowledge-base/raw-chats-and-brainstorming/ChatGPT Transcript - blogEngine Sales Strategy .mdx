With a process, you use your own words. You adapt to the prospect. You stay natural and conversational. You stay in control without sounding rehearsed.

This is what professional sellers do. They don’t memorize lines. They follow a process.

* * *

**1.5. 5-Part Sales Process Overview**
--------------------------------------

The full sales process you’ll learn includes five simple stages:

1.  **Sales Introduction**  
     The first impression, why you’re there, and what’s in it for them.
2.  **Sales Questions**  
     Discovering what matters to the buyer and how to help them.
3.  **Sales Presentation**  
     Matching your product or service to their needs using benefits, not features.
4.  **Closing the Sale**  
     Confirming their interest and helping them move forward.
5.  **Handling Sales Objections**  
     Addressing any concerns that come up—at any stage.

The objection-handling stage is flexible and used whenever a buyer raises an issue. If no objections arise, you move forward without it.

This process has been used by salespeople in many industries and situations. It gives you everything you need to structure your pitch, stay on track, and close more deals.

**Section 2: The Sales Introduction Stage**
===========================================

* * *

**2.1. Goals of a Strong Introduction**
---------------------------------------

The Sales Introduction is the first stage of the sales process. It’s the point where you meet the prospect and begin the conversation.

This part of the sale is short—it may only take a minute—but it’s critical. If the introduction is weak, it becomes much harder to gain trust and move forward. If it’s strong, you build interest and make it easy to continue.

**The two goals of this stage are:**

1.  Grab the prospect’s attention
2.  Move smoothly into the next stage of the process

* * *

**2.2. Key Elements of a Sales Introduction**
---------------------------------------------

There are five key elements to a professional introduction. These are:

1.  Introducing yourself
2.  Introducing your company or product
3.  Explaining the reason for the meeting
4.  Gaining agreement to the sales agenda
5.  Motivating the prospect to continue to the next stage

Each of these elements supports the next, and they work together to start the sales conversation in a clear and professional way.

* * *

### **2.2.1. Introducing Yourself**

This seems simple, but it’s worth doing well.

State your name clearly. Consider adding a title or description that builds trust. For example: “I’m Jordan Wright, an AI automation specialist with aiConnected.”

The way you introduce yourself sets the tone. Choose whether you want to appear technical, professional, friendly, or authoritative, depending on what fits your prospect and product.

Avoid sounding overly casual or overly formal. Keep it clear and purposeful.

* * *

### **2.2.2. Introducing Your Company or Product**

Right after your personal introduction, give a brief line about your company.

Instead of just stating a company name, say something the prospect can recognize or relate to. For example:

> “I work with aiConnected—we help businesses automate content creation and lead generation using simple AI-powered tools.”

This helps the buyer place you in context and start thinking about how your company may be relevant to them.

You can also add a short benefit or result:

> “We’ve helped hundreds of businesses publish professional blog content without ever needing to write a word.”

Keep it short, relevant, and easy to understand.

* * *

### **2.2.3. Explaining the Reason for the Meeting**

This is one of the most important lines in the entire sales process.

Your reason for being there should be stated clearly, and it must be based on **what the prospect might gain** from speaking with you—not on what you’re selling.

Avoid:

> “I wanted to show you our blog service.”

Use instead:

> “I wanted to see if we could help you get more visibility online by publishing expert blog content without adding more to your plate.”

This one line should capture their attention and give them a reason to keep listening.

If possible, tailor the reason to their specific industry or situation.

* * *

### **2.2.4. Gaining Agreement to the Sales Agenda**

This step helps set expectations and gives you permission to continue.

Here’s an example of what to say:

> “If it’s okay with you, I’d like to ask a few quick questions about your business, then show you how our system might help. If it looks like a fit, we can talk next steps—fair enough?”

This does two things:

1.  It tells them exactly what to expect
2.  It helps you learn whether they’re in a position to buy or move forward

This simple agreement will make it easier to close later, because it reduces surprises and keeps both of you aligned.

* * *

### **2.2.5. Motivating the Prospect to Continue**

Before jumping into your questions, use a line that motivates the prospect to come with you into the next stage.

Example:

> “Just so I can be sure this is a good fit for what you need, can I ask a few quick questions about your current setup?”

This may seem like a small detail, but it helps avoid awkward transitions. It gives the buyer a reason to participate and keeps momentum moving forward.

Without this step, many prospects become passive or hesitant.

* * *

**2.3. The Importance of Language and Framing**
-----------------------------------------------

Every word in your introduction should be chosen carefully. A strong introduction is clear, professional, and focused on the prospect.

Avoid vague or filler statements. Speak directly, and lead the conversation with intent.

When done well, the introduction sets the tone for the entire sale.

* * *

**2.4. Link to the Next Stage**
-------------------------------

At the end of your introduction, you should smoothly link to the **Sales Questioning Stage**.

You do this by using a motivator line, followed by your first question.

This keeps the conversation moving and avoids losing the prospect’s attention.

**Section 3: The Sales Questioning Stage**
==========================================

* * *

**3.1. Purpose of This Stage**
------------------------------

The purpose of the Sales Questioning Stage is to find out what the prospect really wants, needs, or is trying to fix. Without this information, it is difficult to make a meaningful presentation or close a sale.

This stage is not about gathering data—it’s about identifying motivations. What are they trying to achieve? What problems do they want to avoid? What matters most to them?

The quality of your questions determines the quality of your presentation.

* * *

**3.2. Three Principles of Good Questioning**
---------------------------------------------

There are three important principles to follow during this stage:

### **3.2.1. Use a Structured Flow**

Don’t ask random questions. Start wide, then go narrow.

Use this order:

*   **Wide Open Questions**  
     Begin with phrases like:  
     “Tell me about…”  
     “Explain how you’re currently handling…”  
     “Give me a sense of…”
*   **Open Questions**  
     Who, what, where, when, how—these help gather details.
*   **Alternative Answer Questions**  
     “To save time, would you prefer weekly content or monthly?”
*   **Closed Questions**  
     Yes or no questions that confirm agreement.  
     “Would that be something you’d want to see more of?”

Use this flow to take the buyer from general to specific.

* * *

### **3.2.2. Keep It Brief**

You don’t want this part of the conversation to drag on. You’ve worked hard to get their attention—don’t lose it now.

Before the call or meeting, write down the **key information you need to present a solution**. This is a short list—not a script.

For blogEngine, your list might include:

*   Do they publish content now?
*   Who handles their marketing?
*   Do they have a WordPress site or other platform?
*   What are their biggest marketing challenges?
*   What kind of clients do they serve?
*   Are they focused on SEO, social media, lead generation?

Use open questions to get these answers naturally.

At the end, always ask:

> “Is there anything I’ve missed or anything else you think I should know before I make a recommendation?”

This question often reveals the most useful insights.

* * *

### **3.2.3. Make It Conversational, Not an Interrogation**

This should feel like a conversation, not a checklist.

If you fire off short, disconnected questions, the prospect has to work harder to stay engaged. That creates friction. Keep the tone friendly and focused.

Start each line of questioning with a wide open question, then go deeper with follow-up questions, and close the loop with confirmation.

* * *

**3.3. Recommended Question Types**
-----------------------------------

Use a combination of question styles to discover real needs:

*   **Wide Open Questions** – Start the dialogue
*   **Open Questions** – Explore the context
*   **Alternative Answer Questions** – Offer choices
*   **Closed Questions** – Gain small commitments

Add in **Dissatisfaction Questions** when appropriate:

> “What do you wish was better about how you’re doing things now?”  
> “Have you tried anything in the past that didn’t work?”

These give you clues about pain points and buying motivation.

* * *

**3.4. Targeted Information Gathering**
---------------------------------------

Make a habit of preparing a short list of topics to explore before your meetings. Don’t write out full questions—just the information you need.

Examples:

*   Current content process
*   Website platform (WordPress, Wix, etc.)
*   Budget comfort zone
*   Main marketing goal
*   Time spent on content creation
*   Willingness to automate

Use the structure to guide them through those topics.

The more relevant information you collect, the more persuasive your presentation will be.

* * *

**3.5. Transition to the Next Stage**
-------------------------------------

Once you’ve gathered what you need, summarize the key points briefly:

> “Thanks—that helps a lot. Based on what you’ve told me, I think we’ve got a solution that could save you time and make your content marketing a lot easier.”

This sets you up to move into the Sales Presentation Stage without a hard shift in tone.

Now the prospect expects to hear something useful—and you’re ready to deliver it.

**Section 4: The Sales Presentation Stage**
===========================================

* * *

**4.1. Goals of a Sales Presentation**
--------------------------------------

This stage is where you match what the buyer told you during the questioning stage with a solution that fits.

The goal of your presentation is to show how your product or service meets the prospect’s specific needs, solves their problems, or helps them reach their goals.

This is not a product overview. It’s a targeted response to what they told you matters.

* * *

**4.2. Presentation Best Practices**
------------------------------------

There are three key techniques used by professional sellers during the presentation stage:

### **4.2.1. Present What Matters to the Buyer**

In the questioning stage, the buyer told you what’s important to them. That’s the starting point of your presentation.

If they talked about saving time, lead with time-saving features.  
If they mentioned SEO visibility, focus on how blogEngine helps them get found.

The buyer has already told you what they care about—present in that same order.

* * *

### **4.2.2. Focus on Benefits, Not Features**

A feature is what the product _has_.  
A benefit is what the customer _gets_ from that feature.

Examples:

*   **Feature:** “You get 3 blog posts every week.”
*   **Benefit:** “You stay visible in front of your audience and never fall behind on content.”
*   **Feature:** “We include an audio version with every article.”
*   **Benefit:** “Your audience can consume your content even when they’re too busy to read, which increases engagement and keeps them on your site longer.”

Always present benefits that are tied to the buyer’s needs.

* * *

### **4.2.3. Follow the Order of Importance the Buyer Gave You**

If the buyer mentioned price, automation, and SEO—present in that order.

When you follow their priorities, you show them that you listened. This builds trust and keeps the presentation relevant.

When you present out of order or overload them with information they didn’t ask for, you create friction and lose focus.

Let the buyer’s answers write your presentation.

* * *

**4.3. The Trial Close**
------------------------

At the end of your presentation, before you move into the final close, use a **trial close**.

This is a soft question that checks if you’ve met their expectations so far.

Examples:

> “Does this sound like it would solve the problem you mentioned earlier?”  
> “Is this the kind of solution you were hoping to find?”  
> “Have I covered everything you need to know so far?”

A positive answer means you’re ready to move to the close.  
A hesitation or concern means there’s an objection or question still to uncover.

The trial close gives you a chance to address anything before asking for a commitment.

* * *

**4.4. Handling Transitions Smoothly**
--------------------------------------

If the prospect responds well to your trial close, the next step is simple:

> “Great. If you’re ready, I can walk you through how we get started.”

If they hesitate, ask:

> “What else would you need to see to feel comfortable moving forward?”

This keeps the conversation flowing. You don’t want to present, then pause, then suddenly switch to a hard close. The goal is to move forward without breaking momentum.
